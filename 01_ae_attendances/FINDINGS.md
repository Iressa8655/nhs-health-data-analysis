# NHS England A&E Performance Analysis
## April 2025 – March 2026 | Type 1 Emergency Departments

**Data source:** NHS England A&E Attendances and Emergency Admissions 2025-26 (12 monthly CSVs)  
**Scope:** 125 Type 1 (major, consultant-led) A&E departments in England  
**Target:** 95% of patients seen within 4 hours (national standard)  
**Analysis date:** April 2026

---

## Executive Summary

England's Type 1 A&E departments spent the entirety of 2025-26 in sustained, system-wide failure against the 95% four-hour standard. The national weighted performance across the year was **60.5%** — a gap of **34.5 percentage points** below target — with **7.1 million patients** waiting more than four hours in a major emergency department over the 12-month period. No month came close to the target. The best month was March 2026 at 63.9%; the worst was January 2026 at 57.1%.

The data tells a story not of seasonal winter pressure, but of **structural, year-round capacity failure**. The variation between the best and worst months (6.8 percentage points) is too small to be explained by winter alone. In the most recent month (March 2026), 120 out of 121 Type 1 trusts are below the 95% target; the single trust meeting it is a specialist children's hospital. Among adult A&E departments, the target is being met by no one.

### The flat line problem

The range between the best and worst month is only **6.8 percentage points**. A system under primarily seasonal pressure would show a pronounced V-shape — strong in summer, collapse in winter, recovery in spring. This data shows almost no V-shape at all. Performance in April (59.8%) is barely different from December (59.4%).

This means the system is already operating at or near its stress ceiling in summer, and winter adds only a marginal extra load on top. The underlying structural capacity problem is present 12 months a year. Seasonal interventions — winter escalation plans, surge beds, bank nurses — are being applied to a problem that is not seasonal. The implication for policy is significant: **you cannot winter-plan your way out of a year-round structural crisis**.

In March 2026:

**Three times as many trusts are worsening as improving.** This is the single most concerning finding in the dataset. The directionality matters: these are not trusts oscillating around a stable baseline. They are trusts that were already struggling and are continuing to deteriorate.

An important note on seasonality: H2 includes the winter months, so some deterioration between H1 and H2 is expected. What distinguishes the trusts flagged here is the *magnitude* of the change — greater than 2 percentage points — and in some cases the fact that they were already critically low at the start of H2.

## 4. Case Study — The Princess Alexandra Hospital: What Does Turnaround Actually Look Like?

Princess Alexandra Hospital (PAH) in Harlow, Essex achieved **+10.6 percentage points** improvement between H1 and H2, improving from 62.0% to 72.7% through winter. NHS England independently cited PAH as **#1 nationally for Type 1 A&E improvement**, with a **23% improvement in December 2025 vs December 2024** — a counter-cyclical performance gain that is exceptionally rare in NHS data.

PAH also recorded the **highest productivity growth of any acute trust in England** in both October 2025 (+14.3%) and November 2025 (+16.8%), and moved from Segment 4 to Segment 3 in the NHS Oversight Framework during this period. This is a nationally recognised turnaround, not a data artefact.

### What drove the improvement

Public sources — NHS England publications, CQC reports, local press, and trust communications — reveal the following contributing factors:

**1. EPR maturation (Oracle Health "Alex Health", live November 2024)**
PAH implemented a new electronic health record in November 2024. The initial go-live is always disruptive. By October 2025, the system had been running for nearly a year: clinicians were familiar with it, the bed management module was providing real-time capacity visibility, and result-chasing delays were reduced. The first winter *with a mature EPR* (H2 2025-26) compared to the first winter *without any EPR* (H2 2024-25) is likely the single strongest explanatory factor.

**2. Physical ED and UTC refurbishment (completed spring 2025)**
Works to improve the Emergency Department and Urgent Treatment Centre layout were completed in spring 2025. PAH's H2 improvement was the first winter in the refurbished physical environment — better patient flow through the department, improved clinical bay configuration, reduced corridor crowding.

**3. New senior clinician streaming process (piloted from September 2025)**
In September 2025, PAH piloted a front-door streaming process: a senior clinician assessing patients at arrival and directing them immediately to the right-fit service — UTC for minor injuries, SDEC for same-day emergency care, full resus pathway for high acuity. This is the NHS Model Emergency Department framework in practice. It avoids placing every patient in a single queue, which is the primary driver of long waits for lower-acuity patients.

**4. Multi-agency UTC partnership model (since November 2023, maturing through 2025)**
PAH restructured its Urgent Treatment Centre as a multi-agency partnership (PAH + Stellar Healthcare + Stort Valley primary care + Essex Partnership + Hertfordshire Community NHS Trust), with scheduled appointment slots reducing walk-in ED pressure. By H2 2025-26, this model was two years embedded and operating at full capacity.

**5. New leadership with explicit improvement targets**
CEO Thom Lafferty joined in November 2024 with a public target of reaching the top 20 nationally. New leadership setting measurable goals and communicating them internally is harder to quantify but consistently associated with NHS performance turnarounds in the literature.

**6. Foundational CQC remedial work, now embedded**
Following a 2023 CQC inspection, PAH was required to implement standardised triage, 15-minute initial observations, and improved nurse staffing. These were embedded requirements by H2 2025-26 and formed the operational foundation on which the more visible improvements were built.

### The key lesson

The PAH story is not one single intervention. It is the **compound effect of multiple overlapping changes**, all landing at roughly the same time:

| Layer | PAH intervention |
|---|---|
| Digital infrastructure | EPR maturation |
| Physical infrastructure | ED/UTC refurbishment |
| Clinical process | Senior clinician streaming |
| System partnership | Multi-agency UTC model |
| Leadership | New CEO, explicit improvement targets |
| Regulatory compliance | CQC remedial actions embedded |

Trusts that implement one layer without the others tend to see smaller or shorter-lived gains. The policy implication is that improvement support packages for struggling trusts need to be **comprehensive and multi-layered**, not narrowly focused on a single lever such as staffing numbers or a new pathway.

## 5. Clinical Perspective — What Do These Numbers Mean for Patients?

The four-hour target is sometimes criticised as an administrative measure that does not reflect clinical reality. The evidence does not support this view.

### Mortality and patient harm

Multiple studies show that patients spending more than four hours in a Type 1 A&E have significantly higher 30-day mortality than equivalent patients seen within four hours, after adjustment for acuity and case mix. The mechanism involves: delayed identification of deteriorating patients in overcrowded departments, stretched nursing ratios reducing the frequency of observations, and delayed escalation in high-noise environments.

### Time-sensitive conditions where delay causes direct harm

| Condition | Treatment window | What delay costs |
|---|---|---|
| STEMI (heart attack) | 90 min door-to-balloon | Each 30-min delay raises 1-year mortality by ~7.5% |
| Ischaemic stroke | 4.5 hrs for thrombolysis | ~1.9 million neurones lost per minute untreated ("time is brain") |
| Sepsis | 1-hour antibiotics (Sepsis Six) | Each hour's delay in antibiotics raises mortality by ~7% |
| Major trauma | "Golden hour" | Haemorrhage control and damage control surgery within 60 min |

When a Type 1 A&E is running at 37–45% four-hour performance (as the bottom ten trusts are in this data), the department is overwhelmed. Staff are managing queues rather than actively seeking and fast-tracking the deteriorating patients who need it most. The patients most harmed by delay are exactly those who cannot afford to wait.

### Exit block — the hidden driver of long A&E waits

A significant proportion of the waits captured in this data are not caused by problems *within* the A&E. They are caused by **exit block**: patients who are medically ready to move to a ward but cannot because no inpatient bed is available. This happens when:
- Ward occupancy exceeds safe levels (NHS acute trusts typically run at 95-99% vs a recommended 85%)
- Medically fit inpatients are waiting for social care packages, CHC assessments, or community step-down beds
- Ambulance crews are unable to hand patients over, creating a queue before the patient even reaches triage

Exit block means that improving A&E performance sustainably requires solving problems *outside* the emergency department — in bed management, discharge planning, and social care commissioning. Operational reviews focused solely on ED processes will treat symptoms rather than causes.

### Mental health presentations

An increasing proportion of Type 1 A&E attendances are for mental health crises — patients in acute distress, with self-harm injuries, or requiring urgent psychiatric assessment. These patients often face the longest waits (median 6-8+ hours at many trusts), do worst in the bright, loud, clinically busy environment of an A&E waiting room, and are among those most at risk of deterioration or self-discharge before being seen. PAH's planned Mental Health Urgent Care Unit (opening summer 2026) directly addresses this — by diverting mental health presentations to a calmer, appropriate clinical space, it both improves care for those patients and reduces congestion in the main department for everyone else. This is an evidence-based model that other trusts in the bottom ten should consider.

### The 7.1 million figure in context

7.1 million four-hour breaches across the year represents approximately **590,000 patients per month** spending more than four hours in a major A&E. For scale, this is equivalent to the entire population of Leeds — every single month, all year.

## 6. Recommendations

### Immediate priorities (0–3 months)

1. **Commission an urgent operational review at University Hospitals Plymouth.** At 37.4% and actively worsening (-10.1pp H1→H2), Plymouth is the highest patient-harm risk trust in this dataset. The review should examine exit block, ambulance handover delays, SDEC utilisation, ward occupancy, and nursing establishment.

2. **Investigate Birmingham Women's and Children's deterioration urgently.** A -10.4pp decline at a specialist children's hospital is clinically and operationally unexpected. Possible causes include paediatric respiratory surge, workforce crisis, or paediatric community discharge pathway failure. This should not be attributed to generic winter pressure.

3. **Convene a regional system review for the Cheshire/North West cluster.** Mid Cheshire, Wirral, Countess of Chester, and East Cheshire are all in the bottom ten — four adjacent trusts underperforming simultaneously. This is a regional system problem (ambulance response, social care, NHS 111 routing) that cannot be solved trust-by-trust.

### Medium-term (3–12 months)

4. **Commission a structured learning review of Princess Alexandra Hospital.** PAH achieved the largest improvement nationally through a replicable, multi-layered model. The specific streaming protocol, EPR configuration, and UTC partnership model should be documented in detail and offered as a supported implementation package to the ten worst-performing trusts.

5. **Address exit block directly.** Set ICB-level targets for discharge of medically fit inpatients within 24 hours. Review social care package decision timelines and CHC assessment backlogs. Consider additional community step-down bed commissioning in ICS areas with the worst-performing A&E clusters.

6. **Engage the 45 worsening trusts now, before next winter.** Improvement initiatives — EPR configuration, physical streaming improvements, community partnership development — take 6-12 months to operationalise. Engagement starting now (April 2026) gives adequate lead time for changes to be embedded by October 2026.

### Strategic (12+ months)

7. **Establish honest interim performance milestones.** The 95% target has not been met nationally since 2015. A credible improvement trajectory with staged milestones (e.g. 70% nationally by April 2027, 80% by April 2028) would be more motivating and more publicly honest than retaining a target that no adult trust currently achieves, and that risks normalising perpetual failure.

## 7. Other NHS Datasets Worth Exploring Alongside This Analysis

This A&E dataset does not stand alone — the following NHS England datasets would significantly enrich the picture: [Statistics » Statistical work areas](https://www.england.nhs.uk/statistics/statistical-work-areas/)

| Dataset | What it adds |
|---|---|
| **Discharge Delays** | Directly quantifies exit block — how many patients are stuck in hospital beds waiting for social care or CHC decisions. Explains *why* A&E is backing up. |
| **Ambulance Quality Indicators** | Captures ambulance response times and handover delays at ED doors. Trusts near the bottom of the A&E table often have the worst ambulance handover data. |
| **Bed Availability and Occupancy** | Shows how full acute wards are, month by month. High occupancy is the direct driver of exit block. |
| **Referral to Treatment (RTT) Waiting Times** | Completes the picture: patients who cannot access outpatient care eventually deteriorate and attend A&E. Long RTT waits and poor A&E performance are often co-located. |
| **Mental Health: CYP Eating Disorder / IAPT** | Mental health access failures upstream contribute to crisis attendances in A&E. |
| **Urgent and Emergency Care Daily Situation Reports** | Real-time daily data during winter — useful for understanding exactly when pressure spikes occur. |
| **Integrated Urgent Care** | NHS 111 call volumes and disposition data. High A&E attendance partly reflects unmet need at 111 and primary care level. |

## Data Quality Notes

- Analysis uses **weighted averages** throughout. A simple average would treat a 5,000-attendance district general the same as a 25,000-attendance teaching hospital; this is not appropriate for national-level analysis.
- The **Period column** in NHS England's monthly CSVs is inconsistently formatted ("TOTAL", "Total", "TOTAL " with trailing space). Specific cleaning logic was required and should be documented for reproducibility.
- The **H1 vs H2 trend comparison** does not fully isolate seasonal effects from genuine deterioration. Trusts worsening due to winter pressure alone will appear alongside those on a genuine downward trajectory. Operational context is needed to distinguish between them.
- This dataset records **attendances and breach counts**, not diagnoses, acuity scores, or outcomes. Clinical harm inferences in Section 5 are based on published academic literature, not this dataset directly.
